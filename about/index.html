Early Outbreak Detection and Monitoring

In December 2013, in the remote village of Meliandou, Guinea, an 18-month-old boy played near a hollow tree in his back yard. He soon became ill, and days later, the young boy died.

Shortly thereafter, his caretakers also fell ill and died. Then, others in the village. Next, the local hospital workers and others in the neighboring city.

On March 20 2014, Guinea acknowledged the presence of a mystery fever outbreak to the public. Two days later, they learned its name: Ebola

International Response

Sadly, international response to this deadly outbreak was slow. The CDC issued their highest alert on August 7. The WHO and UN declared health emergencies on August 8. In the span of 22 weeks between the original Ebola announcement and the declaration of emergency, more than 900 lives had been lost. By the end of the outbreak, more than 11000 people had died.

Our Project

Our outbreak identification and monitoring system brings together multiple global data sources to identify and track disease-related activities around the world. The goal is to provide a rapid identification system through daily monitoring and analysis of web-based news media and social media sources. Users can access a dashboard of current events and quickly view all of them or explore individual events in more detail.

Method

Machine learning models are used to determine the probability that an individual news item is relevant to the subject matter. Then, statistical process control algorithms are employed to identify when there has been a significant change in the number or volume of new items. The results are displayed graphically, and the source data can be accessed through links to the original URLs for further validation.

GDELT Data

Google's GDELT Project monitors broadcast, print, and web news from nearly every country on the globe and in over 100 languages. Their processes identify the people, locations, organizations, and events that are being written about and discussed. We in turn search through the daily GDELT data files in search of articles that are of specific interest, running them through our data model to see how likely they are to be an important contribution to a new or developing event.

Twitter Data

This ubiquitous and familiar microblogging application has the potential to be a rich source of notices on breaking news and events, yet the signal-to-noise ratio can be overwhelming. We have taken an approach of applying a whitelist of known Twitter users who are active participants in global humanitarian and health activities. This narrows the range of communications to search through while also increasing the value of those communications. Future iterations of this system will gradually expand the whitelist based on analyses of the quality of information being received.

WHO Data

The World Health Organization makes available detailed information on public health events around the world. The information is published on a weekly basis and relies on official reports from government entities. These aspects make it less useful for dynamic early identification of events, however it does provide a timely source of information regarding the human impact of such events. We have used their information regarding the ebola outbreaks in Africa to augment our other data sources with information regarding the mortality and morbidity of the disease.

The Result

Ultimately, we analyzed more than 20 million articles and 160000 tweets written between January 1 2014 and November 25 2015 for signs of disease outbreaks. We generated nearly 2 years worth of daily dashboards and informational reports.

Visual Components

  • Shewhart Charts

     

  • How do we know whether the number of articles/tweets that we see each day is normal or unusual? We took a cue from the world of manufacturing.

    Manufacturers use Shewhart charts to detect abnormalities in otherwise reliable processes. We use them to generate alerts when the number of outbreak-predictive articles or tweets is unusually high (more then 3 standard deviations above the previous 30-day average). They also generate warnings when the number of daily articles relevant to a given disease is above average for 6 days in a row.

  • Where in the world is it happening?

    Using the GDELT location data, we can indicate where the article originated. Many of the Twitter accounts that we monitor have location data enabled as well. We plot all of those points on a world map to pinpoint the likely outbreak hotspots for the disease.

  • Map Plots

     

  • Confidence

     

  • How certain are we that the articles we have identified are indicative of potential crisis situations?

    Each time the model designates that an article is potentially indicative of a crisis, it also produces a confidence measurement (a number between 0.5 and 1.0). By looking at how those confidence measurements are distributed, we can get a sense for how confident we should be in the alerts that are being raised.

  • When WHO human impact data are available, we show how the disease has progressed in terms of confirmed cases and confirmed deaths. For this project, we were able to include human impact charts for Ebola during 2015.
